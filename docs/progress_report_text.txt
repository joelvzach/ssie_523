Progress Report: Global Tourism Complexity Simulation
SSIE 523 - Agent-Based Modeling of Global Tourism Dynamics
April 21, 2026

We set out to build an agent-based simulation that demonstrates complexity in global tourism—not as a predictive forecasting tool, but as an exploratory sandbox for understanding how heterogeneous tourist decisions, destination constraints, and external shocks interact to produce emergent patterns. Our work began with a fundamental question: can we reproduce observed tourism dynamics from first principles of individual choice behavior, without hardcoding aggregate outcomes? To ground this effort, we assembled seven major datasets (UN Tourism arrivals, WEF TTDI scores, ACLED conflict events, Numbeo cost indices, WHO air quality, UNESCO heritage sites, and OECD economic indicators) into a unified foundation of 8,911 country-year observations spanning 177 countries over 30 years. Our correlation analysis revealed that TTDI attractiveness explains only 13% of variance in tourism flows (r = 0.364), confirming that traditional econometric approaches capture less than one-seventh of the underlying complexity. This finding motivated our agent-based approach, which treats tourism as an emergent phenomenon arising from heterogeneous decision-making rather than aggregate optimization. We further reviewed six foundational papers (Bertocchi on carrying capacity, Rosselló on disaster elasticities, Škare on pandemic recovery, Peng on business/leisure elasticities, Sönmez & Graefe on return visitors, and Muler González on overtourism thresholds) to ensure our mechanisms were literature-grounded rather than assumed.

Several critical learnings shaped the simulation design. Distance emerged as the dominant factor for Budget (η = 0.34) and Family (η = 0.40) segments, contradicting naive assumptions that budget travelers chase low prices regardless of location. Business travel was embedded within segments (Luxury 40% business probability, Budget 15%, Adventure 5%, Family 0%) rather than modeled separately, producing the observed 11% overall business share while maintaining segment coherence. TFI (Tourism Friendliness Index) was implemented as a destination-state moderator affecting capacity and attractiveness, not as a utility factor, because literature describes resident attitudes as triggering policy responses rather than directly influencing individual tourist choices. We removed Popularity and Social Media factors from the initial 8-factor utility function due to lack of empirical calibration, resulting in a cleaner 6-factor function (Attractiveness, Cost, Crowding, Risk, Distance, Memory) with stronger empirical backing. Trip frequency was modeled as a Poisson process with segment-specific rates rather than fixed schedules, producing realistic variation where some travelers take zero trips in a year while others take multiple.

Our final design prioritizes mechanism clarity over comprehensiveness, focusing on six core mechanisms that demonstrate complex systems concepts taught in this course. Emergence: hub formation arises from distributed softmax choices with segment-specific temperatures (Luxury τ = 0.8 deterministic, Budget τ = 1.2 exploratory), not top-down assignment. Feedback loops: when crowding exceeds 80%, TFI declines at 0.05/day, triggering policy restrictions that reduce effective capacity by 15-30%, creating a balancing loop. Heterogeneity: disaster events apply segment-specific risk multipliers (Family 1.9×, Adventure 1.2×), producing differential behavioral responses. Critical transitions: the 80% capacity threshold triggers qualitative behavioral shifts in visualization and policy response. Path dependence: tourists record satisfaction scores for visited destinations, creating return visitor loyalty (0.55-0.65 probability, matching literature). Geographic friction: distance decay via haversine formula produces realistic regional clustering (Europe 65% intra-regional, Americas 55%). The simulation does not predict the future; it illuminates plausible dynamics under specified conditions, making uncertainty explicit and enabling structured experimentation. We believe this approach—agent-based, empirically grounded, exploratory rather than predictive—offers a more honest and useful tool for understanding complexity in global tourism than traditional forecasting models that claim precision they cannot deliver.
